Democratic reform

Myanmar’s democratic reform efforts have stalled and in some cases have taken a turn for the worse in the past five years. NLD attempts to amend the 2008 constitution to curb the political power of the military and allow Aung San Suu Kyi to become president have been blocked by military lawmakers, who control a quarter of the seats in parliament giving them a veto on amendments. That will remain a formidable obstacle to constitutional change after this election.

Although Suu Kyi once declared that even one political prisoner is one too many, Myanmar has a growing number of what human rights activists consider to be prisoners of conscience. As of June 2020, 641 political prisoners were in various stages of incarceration or prosecution, according to the non-government Association of Assistance for Political Prisoners. Many have been charged under vague national security laws held over from the colonial era and Myanmar’s decades of military rule. Among those jailed have been activists who criticized the military, villagers protesting land grabs or pollution, and journalists accused of defaming government officials or the military. Authorities have also used counter-terrorism and unlawful association laws for prosecutions related to the escalating conflict in Rakhine State.
